When BHP acquired Noront Resources' Ring of Fire project in Ontario for $325 million in 2022, due diligence revealed that 4,800 historical drill holes had massive coverage gaps. High-grade nickel zones showed excellent drill spacing of 25-50 meters, but 78% of the 5,000 km² property had zero drilling within 2 km radius. The sparse zones included geophysical anomalies and favorable geology—potential mineralization completely unexplored due to biased drilling patterns following initial discoveries.

This tutorial covers exploration density analysis: Apache Sedona distributed spatial analytics on Spark loading millions of drill hole coordinates into Delta tables, hexagonal tessellation computing holes per km² across continental-scale portfolios, spatial joins executing in minutes at massive scale (284,000 holes across Western Australia), and automated identification of exploration gaps revealing where billions in spending concentrated and where opportunities remain hidden.

This matters because modern exploration portfolios contain millions of drill holes across hundreds of projects spanning decades. Geologists need to answer: Where have we drilled? What's our actual data coverage? Which zones need infill drilling for resource upgrade?
